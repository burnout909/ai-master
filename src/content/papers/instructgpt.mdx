<StageSection id="intuition" title="① Intuition">

GPT-3 was a remarkable achievement — 175 billion parameters trained on vast swaths of the internet. Yet when you prompted it, it would just as readily produce a toxic rant, a confident hallucination, or a completely off-topic response as a useful answer. The model had learned to predict text, not to be helpful. There was a fundamental mismatch between the training objective (next-token prediction on web data) and the actual goal (follow the user's instruction helpfully and harmlessly).

InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022) introduced the RLHF (Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback) pipeline to bridge this gap. The three-phase recipe is: **(1) Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)** — fine-tune GPT-3 on a small dataset of human-written demonstrations of good responses; **(2) Reward Model (RM)** — train a separate model to score responses by learning from human pairwise preference judgments (which of two responses is better?); **(3) PPO fine-tuning** — use PPO to optimize the SFT model to maximize the RM's score, with a KL-divergence penalty keeping it close to the SFT policy to prevent "reward hacking."

The reward model's core mechanism is the **Bradley-Terry preference model**: given two responses A and B with scalar rewards `r(A)` and `r(B)`, the probability that a human prefers A over B is `sigmoid(r(A) - r(B))`. The larger the margin between the two rewards, the more confident the preference. This is exactly what the widget shows.

<StageSection id="math" title="② Math">

The reward model is trained via maximum likelihood on the **Bradley-Terry preference model**. Given a prompt x, a preferred response `y_w` (winner) and a rejected response `y_l` (loser), the probability that a human picks `y_w` is:

<Math display expr="P(y_w \succ y_l \mid x) = \sigma\!\left(r_\phi(x, y_w) - r_\phi(x, y_l)\right)" />

where `sigma` is the sigmoid function and `r_phi` is the reward model with parameters `phi`. Training minimizes the negative log-likelihood over all collected preference pairs:

<Math display expr="\mathcal{L}_{\text{RM}}(\phi) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x,\,y_w,\,y_l)\sim\mathcal{D}}\!\left[\log \sigma\!\bigl(r_\phi(x, y_w) - r_\phi(x, y_l)\bigr)\right]" />

This is a pairwise log-loss: the model is rewarded for assigning a larger scalar reward to the preferred response and penalized when it cannot distinguish the pair.

The PPO objective adds a KL-divergence penalty against the SFT policy `pi_SFT` to prevent reward hacking:

<Math display expr="\mathcal{L}_{\text{PPO}}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y)\sim\pi_\theta}\!\left[r_\phi(x,y) - \beta\,\mathrm{KL}\!\left(\pi_\theta(y\mid x)\,\|\,\pi_{\text{SFT}}(y\mid x)\right)\right]" />

where `beta` is a hyperparameter controlling how tightly the policy stays near `pi_SFT`. A larger `beta` means more conservative updates; `beta = 0` removes the constraint entirely (risk of reward hacking).
