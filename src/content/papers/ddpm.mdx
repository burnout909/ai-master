<StageSection id="intuition" title="① Intuition">

Diffusion models learn to generate data by learning to **reverse** a gradual noising process. The key insight: if you corrupt an image by adding tiny amounts of Gaussian noise at each of T steps, the result is eventually pure noise. A neural network trained to reverse each small step — to denoise — can then generate novel images by starting from pure noise and denoising T times.

**Setup:** imagine slowly dissolving a photo into static. At step 0 you have the original. At step T (~1000) you have pure Gaussian noise. The model learns to run this film backward.

<StageSection id="math" title="② Math">

**Forward process** — each step adds a small amount of Gaussian noise:

<Math display expr="q(x_t \mid x_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}\!\left(x_t;\; \sqrt{1-\beta_t}\, x_{t-1},\; \beta_t I\right)" />

**Closed-form marginal** — the key algebraic trick: repeated reparameterization collapses T steps into one:

<Math display expr="x_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}\, x_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t}\, \varepsilon, \quad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)" />

where <Math expr="\bar\alpha_t = \prod_{s=1}^{t}(1-\beta_s)" />.

**What should the network predict?** Rather than predicting x_0 directly, Ho et al. found predicting the noise ε gives better results. The loss simplifies to:

<Math display expr="L_\text{simple} = \mathbb{E}_{t, x_0, \varepsilon}\!\left[\bigl\|\varepsilon - \varepsilon_\theta(x_t, t)\bigr\|^2\right]" />
