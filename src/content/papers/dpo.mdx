<StageSection id="intuition" title="① Intuition">

InstructGPT showed that RLHF can align language models to human preferences — but it requires a multi-stage pipeline: first train a reward model (RM) on preference pairs, then run PPO to optimize the policy against the RM while keeping it close to the reference policy via a KL-divergence penalty. That's two separate models, two separate training loops, and a dozen sensitive hyperparameters. If any part is misconfigured, the policy either reward-hacks the RM or barely moves from the SFT baseline.

DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) asks a deceptively simple question: is the reward model even necessary? The key insight is that the KL-regularized RL objective has a **closed-form optimal policy**: `pi*(y|x) ∝ pi_ref(y|x) exp(r(x,y) / beta)`. Inverting that relationship lets you express the reward implicitly as `r(x,y) = beta * log(pi*(y|x) / pi_ref(y|x)) + C(x)`. Plugging this implicit reward back into the Bradley-Terry preference loss causes the partition function `C(x)` to cancel — and what remains is a **simple supervised cross-entropy loss directly on the preference pairs**, parameterized by the policy itself. No explicit RM, no PPO.

  When `log(pi_theta(y_w) / pi_ref(y_w))` is large and positive and `log(pi_theta(y_l) / pi_ref(y_l))` is large and negative, the margin is large, `sigmoid(beta * margin)` approaches 1, and `-log(sigmoid(...))` approaches 0 — very low loss. The policy has correctly pushed probability mass toward winners and away from losers. When both log-ratios are equal the margin is zero, `sigmoid(0) = 0.5`, and the loss is `log(2) ≈ 0.693` — the model is maximally uncertain about the preference.

<StageSection id="math" title="② Math">

The derivation starts from the KL-regularized RL objective that InstructGPT's PPO phase optimizes:

<Math display expr="\max_{\pi_\theta}\; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim \mathcal{D},\, y \sim \pi_\theta(y|x)}\!\left[r(x,y) - \beta\,\mathrm{KL}\!\left(\pi_\theta(y\mid x)\,\|\,\pi_{\text{ref}}(y\mid x)\right)\right]" />

This objective has a closed-form optimal solution. The unique policy that maximizes it is:

<Math display expr="\pi^*(y \mid x) = \frac{1}{Z(x)}\,\pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x)\exp\!\left(\frac{r(x,y)}{\beta}\right)" />

where `Z(x)` is the partition function (normalizing constant). Inverting this gives the implicit reward as a function of the optimal policy:

<Math display expr="r(x,y) = \beta \log \frac{\pi^*(y \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y \mid x)} + \beta \log Z(x)" />

Substituting into the Bradley-Terry preference loss with `C(x) = beta * log Z(x)`:

<Math display expr="\mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x,y_w,y_l)\sim\mathcal{D}}\!\left[\log \sigma\!\left(\beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_w \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_w \mid x)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(y_l \mid x)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(y_l \mid x)}\right)\right]" />

The `C(x)` terms cancel because they appear identically in both the `y_w` and `y_l` terms of the preference difference. What remains depends only on log-ratios that are computable from `pi_theta` and the frozen `pi_ref` — no explicit reward model, no partition function evaluation.
