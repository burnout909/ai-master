<StageSection id="intuition" title="① Intuition">

Before ViT, every competitive image model used **convolutional neural networks**. CNNs encode two strong inductive biases: *locality* (each filter sees only a small neighborhood) and *translation equivariance* (sliding a filter across the image). These biases are powerful — they let a ResNet generalize well even on modest datasets. But they also cap what the architecture can learn: a convolution can never directly relate a pixel in the top-left corner to one in the bottom-right.

ViT asks: what if we stripped away all CNN bias and applied a plain Transformer? The trick is treating the image as a **sequence of patches**. A 224×224 RGB image is cut into 196 non-overlapping 16×16 patches. Each patch is flattened to a 768-dimensional vector and linearly projected — exactly analogous to a word embedding. A learnable CLS token is prepended, learned position embeddings are added, and the resulting 197-token sequence is fed into a standard Transformer encoder. The CLS output is used for classification.

<StageSection id="math" title="② Math">

The two key quantities in ViT are the **sequence length** N and the **token dimension** D:

<Math display expr="N = \frac{H \cdot W}{P^2} \qquad D = P^2 \cdot C" />

where H and W are image height and width, P is the patch size, and C is the number of channels. For the standard ViT-B/16 on 224x224 RGB images: N = 196, D = 768.

The linear patch embedding projects each flattened patch into D dimensions:

<Math display expr="\mathbf{z}_i = \mathbf{x}_i \mathbf{W}_{\text{embed}} + \mathbf{b}_{\text{embed}}, \quad \mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{P^2 C},\; \mathbf{W}_{\text{embed}} \in \mathbb{R}^{P^2 C \times D}" />

Self-attention then operates over the N+1 tokens (including CLS), with complexity:

<Math display expr="\mathcal{O}(N^2 \cdot D)" />

This is manageable at N=196 but grows rapidly with smaller patch sizes (e.g., P=8 gives N=784, roughly 16x more attention operations than P=16).
