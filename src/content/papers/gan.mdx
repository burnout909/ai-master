<StageSection id="intuition" title="① Intuition">

Before GANs, deep generative models were hard to train well. Variational autoencoders required explicit likelihood formulations and blurry samples; energy-based models were unwieldy. In 2014, Goodfellow et al. proposed a radically different idea: pit two networks against each other in a **minimax game**.

The **Generator** G takes random noise z from a prior p_z (e.g. Gaussian) and tries to produce samples indistinguishable from real data. The **Discriminator** D looks at a sample and tries to tell real from fake. Each network is trained to defeat the other. At equilibrium, G produces samples so realistic that D can do no better than random guessing — outputting 0.5 for both real and fake inputs.

<StageSection id="math" title="② Math">

The GAN objective is a minimax value function over the discriminator D and generator G:

<Math display expr="V(G,D) = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_\text{data}}[\log D(x)] \;+\; \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_z}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))]" />

D wants to **maximize** V (correctly label real as real, fake as fake). G wants to **minimize** V (fool D into labeling fakes as real). Written compactly:

<Math display expr="\min_G \max_D \; V(G,D)" />

The first term rewards D for outputting high probabilities on real data. The second term rewards D for outputting low probabilities on generated data (and punishes G for failing to fool D).

Implement the discriminator loss for one training step in NumPy. Given arrays `real_probs` (D's outputs on real data) and `fake_probs` (D's outputs on G's samples), compute the **negative** minimax objective that D minimises (i.e. the binary cross-entropy loss):

```
loss = -mean(log(D(real)) + log(1 - D(fake)))
```
