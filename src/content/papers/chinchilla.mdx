<StageSection id="intuition" title="① Intuition">

Before Chinchilla, the dominant belief about large language model scaling came from Kaplan et al. (2020): parameters matter far more than training tokens. GPT-3 (175B parameters, 300B tokens) was the embodiment of that belief — an enormous model trained on a comparatively small dataset. The Kaplan scaling laws suggested that for a fixed compute budget you should spend almost all of it on parameters, keeping the token count roughly constant.

Hoffmann et al. (2022) ran a much more careful set of scaling experiments and reached a different conclusion. When you hold compute fixed and vary both the number of parameters N and the number of tokens D simultaneously, you find an interior optimum: the loss is minimized when N and D grow at roughly the **same rate** — not 10:1 as Kaplan implied, but close to 1:1. The paper named their compute-optimal 70B model "Chinchilla" and showed it outperforms the 280B Gopher model on almost every benchmark — using exactly the same compute budget — simply because Gopher was trained on too few tokens.

<StageSection id="math" title="② Math">

The Chinchilla paper fits a parametric model to the observed training loss across hundreds of runs:

<Math display expr="L(N, D) = E + \frac{A}{N^{\alpha}} + \frac{B}{D^{\beta}}" />

Here E is the irreducible entropy of natural language, A/N^alpha captures the contribution from model size, and B/D^beta captures the contribution from dataset size. The fitted constants are approximately A = 406, B = 410, alpha = 0.34, beta = 0.28, E = 1.70.

Given a fixed compute budget C, the total training FLOPs are approximated as:

<Math display expr="C \approx 6 N D" />

so D = C / (6N). Substituting into L(N, D) and minimising over N (equivalently over D, since C is fixed) yields:

<Math display expr="N^* \propto C^{a}, \quad D^* \propto C^{b}, \quad a = \frac{\beta}{\alpha + \beta} \approx 0.46, \quad b = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta} \approx 0.54" />

Both exponents are near 0.5, confirming the near-symmetric scaling. The ratio D*/N* = 20 follows from plugging the fitted A, B, alpha, beta values into the first-order condition.
