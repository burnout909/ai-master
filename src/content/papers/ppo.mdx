<StageSection id="intuition" title="① Intuition">

Policy gradient methods (REINFORCE and its variants) update network parameters in the direction that makes high-reward actions more likely. The problem is step size: a single gradient update can move the policy so far from where the data was collected that the new policy behaves entirely differently — performance collapses, and you can never recover because the old data is no longer representative of the new policy. This is the "destructively large update" problem.

TRPO (Trust Region Policy Optimization, 2015) solved it by adding a hard KL-divergence constraint: the new policy may not be more than a fixed distance from the old one in probability space. The constraint ensures safe, monotonically improving updates. But enforcing it requires computing second-order derivatives (a conjugate-gradient solve on the Fisher information matrix) — expensive, tricky to implement, and hard to combine with architectures that share parameters between the policy and value networks.

PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) achieves the same trust-region effect with a single line of algebra. Define the probability ratio `r_t = pi_new(a|s) / pi_old(a|s)`. If the policy improves an action that already has `r > 1 + eps`, the additional gradient contribution is simply zeroed out — the **clip** stops rewarding the policy for straying further. If the policy is making a bad action less likely but `r` has already dropped below `1 - eps`, the same clipping applies. Result: a first-order optimizer (Adam) with trust-region-like safety.

<StageSection id="math" title="② Math">

The vanilla policy gradient surrogate — sometimes called the CPI (conservative policy iteration) objective — is:

<Math display expr="L^{\text{CPI}}(\theta) = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t \left[ \frac{\pi_\theta(a_t | s_t)}{\pi_{\theta_\text{old}}(a_t | s_t)} \hat{A}_t \right] = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t \left[ r_t(\theta)\, \hat{A}_t \right]" />

where `r_t(theta) = pi_new(a|s) / pi_old(a|s)` is the probability ratio and `A_hat_t` is the estimated advantage. Maximizing `L_CPI` without constraint would move the policy arbitrarily far for high-advantage actions.

PPO introduces the **clipped surrogate**:

<Math display expr="L^{\text{CLIP}}(\theta) = \hat{\mathbb{E}}_t \left[ \min\!\left( r_t(\theta)\,\hat{A}_t,\; \operatorname{clip}(r_t(\theta),\, 1-\varepsilon,\, 1+\varepsilon)\,\hat{A}_t \right) \right]" />

The typical value is eps = 0.2. The min of the two terms makes `L_CLIP` a **lower bound** on `L_CPI` (for positive advantages): the clipped version is always as small or smaller than the unclipped version when r exceeds the boundary.

    """

    advantages: (N,) estimated advantages A_hat_t
    eps:        clip parameter (default 0.2)
    returns:    scalar mean clipped objective (to be maximized)
