<StageSection id="intuition" title="① Intuition">

Before 2012, training deep neural networks with many layers was nearly impossible. The culprit: **saturating activations**. Both tanh and sigmoid squash their outputs into a bounded range, and once you get far from zero — say, x = 5 or x = −5 — the derivative collapses toward zero. Multiply those near-zero gradients across 8 layers and the signal vanishes long before it reaches early weights.

**Setup:** play with the widget below. Set x = 5, then cycle through sigmoid → tanh → ReLU. Watch f′(x).

<PredictThenReveal client:load predictPrompt="Before touching the widget: at x = 5, what do you expect f′(x) to be for tanh vs ReLU? Write your guess, then check.">
  At x = 5, tanh′(5) ≈ 0.0001 — effectively zero. Sigmoid′(5) ≈ 0.0066 — also nearly dead. But ReLU′(5) = **1.0**, always. That is the entire story of why deep networks became trainable. AlexNet stacked 8 layers (5 conv + 3 FC), 60 million parameters, trained on two GTX 580 GPUs. With tanh they could not have gotten the gradients through; with ReLU they won ImageNet 2012 by 10.9 percentage points over the next best entry.
</PredictThenReveal>

<StageSection id="math" title="② Math">

**Three activation functions:**

ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit):

<Math display expr="f(x) = \max(0,\, x), \qquad f'(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & x > 0 \\ 0 & x \le 0 \end{cases}" />

Tanh:

<Math display expr="f(x) = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}}, \qquad f'(x) = 1 - \tanh^2(x)" />

Sigmoid:

<Math display expr="f(x) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-x}}, \qquad f'(x) = f(x)\bigl(1 - f(x)\bigr)" />

**Why tanh gradient vanishes — derivation:**

The tanh derivative peaks at x = 0 where tanh(0) = 0, giving f′(0) = 1 − 0² = 1. As |x| grows, tanh(x) → ±1, so:

<Math display expr="f'(x) = 1 - \tanh^2(x) \;\to\; 1 - 1 = 0 \quad \text{as } |x| \to \infty" />

**Softmax output layer:**

AlexNet's final layer outputs a probability distribution over 1000 ImageNet classes using softmax:

<Math display expr="\text{softmax}(z)_j = \frac{e^{z_j}}{\sum_{k=1}^{K} e^{z_k}}" />

<DerivationStep client:load

      { text: "They sum to K (number of classes)", correct: false, explain: "Dividing by the sum of all e^z_k ensures the total is exactly 1." },
