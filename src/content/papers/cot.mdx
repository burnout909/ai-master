<StageSection id="intuition" title="① Intuition">

By 2022 large language models like GPT-3 had shown impressive in-context learning on many tasks. But on multi-step arithmetic and commonsense reasoning benchmarks, even a 540B-parameter model would fail by jumping straight to a wrong answer. Fine-tuning helped little: the bottleneck was not knowledge but the inability to *decompose* hard problems into smaller steps. Simply scaling up further did not fix this — at 540B params, GSM8K accuracy with standard prompting sat around 18%.

Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting changes the prompt format rather than the model weights. Instead of showing the model `(question, answer)` pairs in the few-shot prompt, you show `(question, reasoning steps, answer)` triplets. The model then imitates this format on the query — first writing a chain of reasoning, then writing the final answer. That single format change pushed GSM8K accuracy from ~18% to ~57% on PaLM 540B. Remarkably, it only helps at roughly 60B+ parameters; smaller models produce incoherent chains that hurt accuracy.

<StageSection id="math" title="② Math">

Standard few-shot prompting maximizes the probability of the answer directly:

<Math display expr="p_\theta(a \mid x)" />

where `x` is the question and `a` is the answer. Chain-of-thought prompting instead factors generation through an intermediate reasoning chain `r`:

<Math display expr="p_\theta(a \mid x) = \sum_r p_\theta(a \mid r,\, x)\; p_\theta(r \mid x)" />

In practice the model generates a single high-probability chain `r*` (greedy or beam search), then extracts the answer:

<Math display expr="\hat{a} = \arg\max_a\; p_\theta\!\left(a \mid r^*, x\right), \quad r^* = \arg\max_r\; p_\theta(r \mid x)" />

The reasoning chain `r*` decomposes the hard compositional problem into a sequence of simpler local steps, each of which is individually more tractable for the model. In the marginal `p(a | r*, x)`, the answer is now conditioned on a context that has already resolved the intermediate quantities.
